Write a policy research brief with the following structure:

1. **Policy Issue Summary** — 2-3 sentences precisely defining the policy problem, why it matters, and the current decision-making context.

2. **Factual and Empirical Foundation** — The verified factual record: what the data show, what the current legal and regulatory status is, and what has changed recently. Flag any facts the fact-checker identified as unverified or disputed.

3. **Historical and Comparative Context** — Key historical precedents and international comparisons with direct relevance to current policy options. Highlight transferable lessons and important contextual differences.

4. **Stakeholder and Political Landscape** — Who the key actors are, what they want, and where the genuine points of political and normative conflict lie.

5. **Policy Options and Trade-offs** — The major policy approaches under consideration or available, with the key trade-offs for each. Where do analysts agree? Where is there genuine expert disagreement and why?

6. **Evidentiary Assessment** — What the peer-reviewed literature and policy research say. Note the strength of evidence, methodological limitations, and where research is thin or contested.

7. **Analytical Gaps and Further Research** — What questions remain unanswered? What additional evidence would be needed for confident policy recommendations? What is rapidly evolving and may change the picture?

Incorporate the fact-checker's findings throughout. Flag claims identified as unverified or inconsistent. Do not smooth over contradictions between agents — where analysts disagree, represent the disagreement and its implications clearly. Structure the brief so it can serve as a foundation for academic policy writing.
