Write a historical research brief with the following four-section structure. This structure is MANDATORY — establish the historical foundation completely before reaching modern implications.

1. **Foundational Brief** — The anchor event, verified and contextualized. What happened, when, why, and in what environment. Present the event on its own terms before any retrospective analysis. Include the era context — the political, economic, and social conditions that made this event possible. This section should give a reader who knows nothing about the event a complete, accurate understanding of what occurred and why it mattered at the time. Draw from the Historical Anchor, Era Context, and Primary Source agents.

2. **Causal Arc** — The cause-and-effect chain from the anchor event through each era to the present. Structure chronologically: immediate aftermath, short-term consequences, medium-term structural changes, and long-term legacy. For each causal link, state the mechanism — how cause produced effect. Flag where causal claims are well-evidenced vs. where they are scholarly inference. Include the counterfactual analysis where it illuminates why the actual path was significant. Draw from the Causal Chain, Ripple Timeline, and Counterfactual agents.

3. **Present-Day Resonance** — What persists, what has been reversed, and what has evolved. Connect the causal arc to specific current conditions: active legislation, controlling precedent, institutional structures, political dynamics, and cultural discourse. Be specific — name current laws, pending cases, active debates. Draw from the Modern Impact, Government Docs, and Facts agents. Clearly label each finding as PERSISTS, EVOLVED, or REVERSED.

4. **Open Historical Questions** — What scholarship hasn't resolved, what's actively debated, what new evidence might change the picture, and what aspects of the event's legacy are currently unstable or likely to shift. Include both historiographic debates (scholars disagree) and contemporary policy uncertainties (the legacy's future is contested). Draw from the Scholarly Consensus and Research Review agents.

Incorporate the fact-checker's findings throughout. Flag claims identified as unverified or inconsistent. Do not smooth over contradictions between agents — where analysts disagree, represent the disagreement. Do not import modern values into the foundational brief — describe the era first, evaluate it second.
