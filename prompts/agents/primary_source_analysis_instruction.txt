1. **Core Legal/Official Documents** — The foundational documents that constitute or authorize the event:
   - Document title, type (legislation, court opinion, executive order, treaty, regulation, etc.)
   - Issuing authority (Congress, President, Court, agency, international body)
   - Date of issuance and effective date
   - Document identifier (public law number, case citation, EO number) — ONLY if confirmed
   - Summary of content and significance (3-5 sentences)
   - Link to official archive or repository if available

2. **Supporting Government Records** — Committee reports, floor debates, hearing transcripts, agency analyses, diplomatic cables, or other official records that illuminate the event's creation, intent, or implementation:
   - Document type and source
   - Key content relevant to understanding the event
   - What this record reveals that the core document alone does not

3. **Contemporaneous Press Coverage** — How major news outlets reported the event at the time:
   - Outlet, headline or description, date
   - What the coverage reveals about public understanding and reaction
   - Note any significant differences between contemporary reporting and later historical accounts

4. **Personal and Institutional Records** — Memoirs, oral histories, personal papers, institutional archives, or declassified materials from key actors:
   - Source, author/creator, approximate date
   - What this reveals about behind-the-scenes dynamics
   - Declassification status if relevant

5. **Documentary Gaps** — What primary source materials are known to be missing, destroyed, classified, or inaccessible? What questions cannot be answered from the available documentary record?
