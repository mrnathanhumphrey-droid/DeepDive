Your job is to read all of the above and answer one question: **Is this research accurate, and can a graduate student trust it?**

Work through the following verification checks:

1. **Factual Accuracy** — Go claim by claim through the synthesis and agent outputs. For each significant factual assertion: Is it correct? Is it consistent with what the other agents say? Does it match what the cited source actually states? Flag any claim that is wrong, overstated, understated, or outdated. Be specific — name the claim, identify the error, and explain what the correct information is.

2. **Citation and Source Integrity** — Are the sources cited real and correctly described? Does the cited source actually support the claim it is attached to? Flag any instance where a source is misrepresented, where a claim is attributed to a source that does not support it, or where a citation appears fabricated. Do not give the benefit of the doubt — if a citation cannot be verified as accurate, flag it.

3. **Statistical and Data Accuracy** — Are all numbers, percentages, dates, and quantitative claims correct? Are statistics from the right time period? Are they being interpreted correctly (e.g., not confusing correlation with causation, not misreading a percentage point change as a percentage change)? Flag any figure that appears wrong, stale, or misinterpreted.

4. **Legal Accuracy** — If the topic involves court decisions, legislation, or regulatory actions: Are case names, holdings, and vote counts correct? Are statutes correctly described? Is the current legal status accurately represented? Are any rulings described as holdings that were actually dicta, plurality opinions, or dissents? Flag any legal misstatement precisely.

5. **Logical Consistency** — Do the conclusions follow from the evidence presented? Are there internal contradictions within the synthesis or between agent outputs where both cannot be correct? Are causal claims supported or are they inferential leaps? Flag any conclusion that is not supported by the evidence in the outputs.

6. **Currency and Recency** — Is the information up to date? Are there claims that were once true but have since changed (laws amended, policies reversed, officials replaced, data superseded)? Flag any claim where recency is a concern and more current information should be sought.

7. **Missing Critical Facts** — Are there facts that are essential to an accurate understanding of this topic that are absent from the research? A factually accurate output that omits a crucial countervailing fact is still misleading. Flag significant omissions that would change the student's understanding.

8. **Chronological Integrity** (HISTORICAL TOPICS ONLY — skip for non-historical analyses) — Are all events in the correct chronological order? Does any agent imply that the anchor event was caused by, influenced by, or responding to something that happened AFTER it? Does any agent import modern terminology, values, or institutional descriptions into era-specific analysis without labeling them as modern framing? Anachronism — reasoning backward through the anchor event — is the single most dangerous failure mode in historical analysis. Flag every instance precisely.

9. **Causal Overreach** (HISTORICAL TOPICS ONLY — skip for non-historical analyses) — Does any agent assert a cause-and-effect link without citing the mechanism by which cause produced effect? "X happened after Y" is not the same as "X happened because of Y." For every causal claim in the synthesis and agent outputs, verify: (a) Is the causal direction correct (forward from anchor, not backward)? (b) Is a specific mechanism identified (legislative response, judicial interpretation, institutional adaptation, etc.)? (c) Is there evidence supporting the mechanism, or is it just temporal adjacency? (d) Does the agent acknowledge when multiple causes contributed, or does it over-attribute to the anchor event alone? Flag every causal claim that asserts connection without evidence of mechanism.
