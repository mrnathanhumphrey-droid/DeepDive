# Policy Context: Congressional Overnight Voting on Surveillance and Funding Legislation

## Historical Policy Background

The current pattern of Congressional overnight voting sessions on surveillance and funding legislation reflects an institutional crisis that has intensified since the 1970s. The practice emerged from the Senate's "vote-a-rama" procedures during budget reconciliation but has expanded to other contentious legislation, creating what critics describe as governance by exhaustion.

The surveillance component traces directly to post-Watergate intelligence reforms. The 1975-1976 Church Committee investigations exposed extensive domestic surveillance overreach, leading to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act of 1978, which established judicial oversight of intelligence collection. However, the 9/11 attacks reversed this trajectory. The USA PATRIOT Act's rushed passage in October 2001—with limited debate and most members admitting they hadn't read the full text—established the precedent for emergency surveillance expansions under time pressure.

Section 702 of FISA, enacted in 2008 through the FISA Amendments Act, authorized warrantless collection of foreign communications that incidentally captures Americans' communications. The program faced little public scrutiny until Edward Snowden's 2013 revelations exposed the scope of NSA bulk collection. Since then, Section 702 reauthorizations have become increasingly contentious, with temporary extensions replacing the multi-year renewals that characterized earlier surveillance legislation.

Internationally, the pattern of emergency legislative procedures during perceived crises has troubling precedents. The Weimar Republic's use of Article 48 emergency decrees (1930-1933) demonstrated how accelerated parliamentary procedures can undermine deliberative democracy. More recently, France's response to the 2015 Paris attacks normalized temporary surveillance measures through expedited procedures, culminating in the 2017 Internal Security Law that made permanent what were intended as emergency powers.

## Comparative Policy Lessons

The contrast between U.S. emergency procedures and peer democracies' approaches to surveillance legislation reveals significant policy lessons. The United Kingdom's 2016 Investigatory Powers Act, despite earning the nickname "Snooper's Charter," was debated in Parliament over 18 months with structured committee review. Germany's post-2016 surveillance expansion through the Federal Intelligence Service Act required Constitutional Court review before implementation. France's 2015 Intelligence Act faced Constitutional Council scrutiny that struck down provisions lacking adequate judicial oversight.

These examples demonstrate that security legislation can advance through normal democratic procedures while maintaining institutional safeguards. Canada's 2015 Anti-Terrorism Act (Bill C-51) faced parliamentary opposition but was debated over months, not hours, allowing for substantive amendments and public input. Australia's rapid passage of the 2018 Assistance and Access Act, while more similar to U.S. emergency procedures, triggered ongoing international business concerns about data security that continue to affect Australia's technology sector.

The European experience suggests that constitutional review mechanisms serve as crucial circuit breakers that prevent emergency procedures from becoming normalized governance. The absence of equivalent review during U.S. temporary extensions creates path dependency toward increasingly expansive surveillance authorities without adequate oversight.

However, direct transferability of foreign models faces significant constraints in the U.S. system. Parliamentary democracies can ensure majority passage while maintaining deliberative periods, but the U.S. separation of powers system creates different incentive structures. The filibuster in the Senate, combined with intense partisan polarization, makes the extended deliberation seen in European systems politically difficult to sustain.

## US-International Divergences

The United States increasingly diverges from international democratic norms in several critical areas. While peer democracies typically implement surveillance expansions through normal legislative procedures with constitutional review, the U.S. has normalized temporary extensions and emergency procedures that bypass deliberative oversight. This creates operational problems for intelligence cooperation with Five Eyes partners, who require stable legal frameworks for joint operations.

The $70 billion immigration enforcement expansion also diverges from international practice. Under the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol, signatory states must maintain non-refoulement principles even during enhanced enforcement. The UN High Commissioner for Refugees has warned that rapid enforcement expansion without corresponding due process safeguards violates international refugee law. Mexico's Foreign Ministry has protested similar funding increases as militarizing border relations, creating diplomatic complications absent in countries with more measured approaches to border security.

The EU's 2020 Schrems II decision invalidating Privacy Shield demonstrates how U.S. surveillance law instability creates broader international economic consequences. European data protection requirements demand predictable legal frameworks, making temporary FISA extensions problematic for transatlantic business relationships and intelligence sharing.

## Institutional and Path Dependency Constraints

Several structural factors constrain current policy options and perpetuate the crisis governance pattern. The Senate filibuster requires 60 votes for most legislation, making comprehensive surveillance reform politically difficult during periods of partisan polarization. This creates incentives for temporary extensions that maintain controversial authorities while avoiding the supermajority required for substantial changes.

The appropriations process itself has become dysfunctional, with continuing resolutions and omnibus bills replacing regular order. This forces complex policy decisions into must-pass legislation under artificial deadlines, preventing the kind of deliberative review that surveillance and immigration policy complexity demands. Intelligence community institutional interests also create path dependency. The federal court system's limited role in reviewing surveillance programs—particularly the secret FISA Court's ex parte proceedings—removes judicial checks that exist in other democracies.
