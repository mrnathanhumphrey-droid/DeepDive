## 3. Historical and Comparative Context

The secondary sanctions framework traces to the Iran-Libya Sanctions Act of 1996 (later renamed the Iran Sanctions Act), establishing U.S. authority to penalize third-party entities for prohibited transactions. The specific targeting of Chinese independent refineries emerged during the Obama administration's 2010-2015 comprehensive Iran sanctions campaign, when these smaller, privately-owned facilities proved more willing than state-owned giants to accept sanctions exposure for profitable Iranian crude transactions.

The current legal architecture stems from the Trump administration's 2018 withdrawal from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) and reimposition of maximum pressure sanctions. Executive Order 13902, signed in January 2020, specifically targets Iran's petroleum sector and provides the authority for the current enforcement action.

### Comparative International Approaches
European responses to U.S. secondary sanctions have centered on the EU Blocking Statute, designed to shield European companies from extraterritorial enforcement, though this has proven largely ineffective due to dollar-system dependencies. Russia's adaptation to energy sanctions following 2022 provides parallels for sanctions evasion methodologies, including "ghost fleets" of aging tankers and complex ownership structures that Iran has adopted.

India's experience securing temporary U.S. waivers for Iranian oil imports through diplomatic engagement demonstrates how countries with significant strategic value can negotiate exemptions, contrasting with China's more confrontational approach of directly challenging U.S. enforcement authority.

## 5. Policy Options and Trade-offs

### Maximum Pressure Expansion
**Approach:** Systematic targeting of all Chinese entities facilitating Iranian energy trade, including state-owned enterprises and financial institutions.
**Trade-offs:** Higher deterrent effect and revenue denial to Iran, but risks broader U.S.-China economic confrontation and potential acceleration of dollar-alternative payment systems.

### Selective Enforcement with Diplomatic Engagement
**Approach:** Maintain sanctions on military-linked entities while providing exemptions for purely commercial transactions, combined with renewed JCPOA negotiations.
**Trade-offs:** Preserves sanctions architecture while creating space for diplomatic resolution, but may signal U.S. enforcement weakness and provide Iran sanctions relief without behavioral change.

### Multilateral Coordination
**Approach:** Build European and Asian partner consensus for coordinated Iran energy sanctions, reducing unilateral enforcement burden.
**Trade-offs:** Enhances legitimacy and effectiveness through broader participation, but requires compromise with allies who prioritize commercial relationships over sanctions enforcement.

Sanctions advocates agree that secondary enforcement creates meaningful economic pressure on Iran.
