# Contextual Background: Transportation Funding Innovation Amid Global Energy Volatility

## Historical Policy Background

Oregon's May 2026 gas tax referendum emerges from a century-long evolution in American transportation finance that began with Oregon's pioneering 1919 gasoline tax—the nation's first state fuel tax dedicated to highway funding. This "user pays" principle became the foundation for both state and federal transportation finance, culminating in the 1956 federal gas tax that funded the Interstate Highway System.

The current crisis reflects structural problems that emerged during previous economic disruptions. The 1973 oil crisis forced states to confront how fuel price volatility and improving vehicle efficiency undermined gas tax revenue stability. The 2008 financial crisis again highlighted transportation budget vulnerability to economic shocks, while the federal gas tax has remained frozen at 18.4 cents per gallon since 1993, creating a widening infrastructure funding gap.

Globally, the 2020s have witnessed widespread fuel tax revolts that fundamentally altered the political landscape for transportation funding. France's **Gilets Jaunes** movement in November 2018—triggered by a proposed 6.5 cent per liter fuel tax increase—escalated into the nation's most severe civil unrest since 1968, forcing President Macron to abandon the tax entirely. Similar patterns emerged across Ecuador (2019), Chile (2019), and the Netherlands (2019-2022), establishing fuel tax increases as politically toxic during periods of economic stress.

The COVID-19 pandemic temporarily suppressed resistance due to reduced mobility and lower oil prices, but geopolitical conflicts have reignited tensions. Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine created global energy price volatility that reinforced political resistance to additional fuel taxation across most democracies.

## Comparative Policy Lessons

International experience reveals stark contrasts in fuel tax politics and alternative funding mechanisms. **Successful models** demonstrate that context and implementation matter critically:

- **Norway** maintains Europe's highest fuel taxes (approximately $2.50 per gallon in taxes) despite high fuel prices, succeeding through sovereign wealth fund redistribution and transparent infrastructure spending that creates visible public benefits.
- **Singapore** transitioned from fuel taxes to comprehensive road pricing, generating stable infrastructure revenue while managing traffic congestion—though this model requires technological infrastructure and political systems incompatible with most democratic contexts.
- **Switzerland** uses direct democracy similar to Oregon's referendum process, with voters consistently approving infrastructure measures when clearly linked to visible improvements.

**Failed attempts** illustrate the political constraints:
- Belgium reversed federal fuel tax increases in 2020-2021 after truckers' strikes paralyzed supply chains.
- Canada's federal carbon tax faced sustained provincial resistance from 2019-2021, with several provinces unsuccessfully challenging it in courts.

**Alternative strategies** have emerged as democracies retreat from fuel taxation:
- Germany shifted to debt-financed infrastructure investment through federal "Future Package" programs rather than fuel tax increases.
- The United Kingdom has frozen fuel duty since 2011, turning to general taxation and borrowing for road maintenance.
- Australia uses mining royalties and general revenue rather than fuel taxes for highway funding in resource-rich states.

Within the United States, recent precedents show mixed results. Washington State's rejected gas tax increase in 2002 demonstrated voter resistance during economic uncertainty, while successes in Indiana (2017) and South Carolina (2017) occurred during periods of economic growth and lower fuel prices. California's 2017 gas tax increase (SB 1) survived a 2018 repeal effort by emphasizing specific road and bridge projects, benefiting from strong Democratic voter turnout.

## US-International Divergences

Oregon's approach represents a significant counter-trend to global fuel tax retreat. While most democracies have frozen or reduced fuel taxes since 2022, Oregon Democrats advanced increases during elevated prices—testing whether local infrastructure needs can overcome broader resistance to carbon pricing mechanisms.

This divergence reflects deeper institutional differences. The U.S. federal system allows state-level policy experimentation impossible in more centralized democracies, while Oregon's Progressive Era direct democracy traditions (established 1902) provide mechanisms for contentious fiscal policy that many countries lack. However, this also creates the political vulnerability demonstrated in France and other countries, where fuel tax increases triggered broader anti-government sentiment.

The international retreat from fuel taxation complicates achieving Paris Agreement targets, as noted by the OECD, since fuel taxes serve dual purposes: revenue generation and carbon reduction through price signals. Oregon's referendum tests whether this dual function remains politically viable in developed democracies.

## Institutional and Path Dependency Constraints

Several structural factors constrain Oregon's policy options and influence the referendum's dynamics. Federal policy gaps create pressure for state action: the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 provided significant one-time funding but did not address long-term Highway Trust Fund sustainability, forcing states to fill the gap through higher taxes.

Oregon's direct democracy heritage creates both opportunities and constraints. The 1902 constitutional amendments enable ballot measures for contentious fiscal policy, as demonstrated in previous tax battles (Measure 5 property tax limitations in 1990, Measures 66 and 67 income tax increases in 2010). However, research consistently shows lower turnout elections favor organized opposition, potentially disadvantaging tax increases moved from November general elections to May primaries.

The regressive nature of gas taxes creates particular political challenges for Democratic sponsors, as these taxes consume higher percentages of income for lower-earning households. This tension between progressive political identity and regressive taxation mechanisms reflects broader challenges facing Democratic-controlled states funding public infrastructure.

Path dependency in transportation funding also constrains alternatives. The century-old "user pays" principle through gas taxes created institutional arrangements and political expectations difficult to modify quickly, even as electric vehicle adoption and fuel efficiency improvements undermine the revenue base.

## Current Relevance

The referendum's timing amid what coverage describes as an "Iran war" driving current fuel price spikes demonstrates how global events intersect with local policy choices, creating political vulnerability beyond the measure's merits. This parallels how the 1979 Iran hostage crisis influenced energy policy debates across multiple election cycles.

Oregon's experience will likely influence similar debates nationwide, as states confront $2.6 trillion in infrastructure needs identified by the American Society of Civil Engineers. The measure's outcome may provide a template for other states or serve as a cautionary tale about the political limits of state-level transportation funding increases.

International development finance institutions, including the World Bank and Asian Development Bank, have identified fuel tax political resistance as a major obstacle to sustainable infrastructure funding globally. Oregon's outcome may influence their advisory recommendations to client states attempting similar reforms.

The critical difference in Oregon's case appears to be explicit linkage between tax increases and road improvement promises, combined with timing during elevated fuel prices that may paradoxically make infrastructure needs more politically salient than tax resistance. This approach reflects lessons from successful infrastructure campaigns that emphasize concrete benefits over abstract fiscal policy—though it directly contradicts the global trend toward fuel tax retreat during energy price volatility.

The measure's complexity—combining gas tax increases with vehicle fees and payroll taxes—represents emerging state-level strategies to diversify transportation revenue streams, following successful comprehensive approaches in Texas and Virginia that gained broader political coalition support than single-mechanism proposals.

**Transferability Limitations**:
Oregon's unique combination of no sales tax, high dependence on vehicle transportation, and populist referendum traditions limits direct comparison applicability.

## 7. Key Uncertainties

**Critical Variables**:
- **Fuel price trajectory**: Sustained high prices increase political resistance but also highlight infrastructure financing needs
- **Electric vehicle adoption rate**: Faster adoption accelerates gas tax revenue decline
- **Federal infrastructure policy**: Changes in federal transportation funding alter state financing requirements

**Model Sensitivity**:
Economic analysis depends heavily on assumptions about:
- Price elasticity of fuel demand (estimates range from -0.2 to -0.8 in the literature)
- Infrastructure investment multiplier effects
- Voter response to tax increases during economic stress

**Alternative Scenarios**:
If crude oil prices decline significantly before the May 19 vote, political dynamics could shift dramatically in favor of the tax increase. Conversely, further price increases could generate overwhelming opposition regardless of infrastructure needs.

The analysis reveals genuine economic trade-offs without clear theoretical dominance by any single framework. The policy's success depends critically on voter evaluation of immediate costs against long-term benefits under conditions of economic uncertainty.
